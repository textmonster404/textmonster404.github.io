CO2

Carbon dioxide (Chemical name CO2) is a colorless gas with a density about 60% higher than dry air. It consists of a carbon atom covalently double bonded to two oxygen atoms. It occurs naturally in Earth's atmosphere as a trace gas. Carbon dioxide can be used as a fire extinguisher by smothering the flames. Carbon dioxide doesn't have a liquid form. it goes straight from gas to solid. The solid form of CO2 is known as dry ice. CO2 is dissolved in water to form carbonated water, used in soft drinks.


Simple Explaination

CO2 is a very important gas. It is made out of one carbon atom and two oxygen atoms. It is a greenhouse gas, meaning that it contributes to the greenhouse effect. Plants use CO2 in photosynthesis. When they do this, it creates many byproducts, such as oxygen. This oxygen is then used by animals to breath. They in turn, breathe out CO2, and the cycle repeats. This is known as the Carbon Cycle.

Carbon dioxide can be found in

  • rocks,
  • animals,
  • plants,
  • water,
  • and the air.

Effects of Carbon Dioxide

When things like fossil fuels are burned for electricity, carbon dioxide is created as a byproduct. This can get into the atmosphere. When carbon dioxide gets into the atmosphere, it can trap heat. This is because it is a greenhouse gas. This causes global warming. Data from ice cores tells us that our atmosphere hasn't had this much carbon dioxide in at least 420000 years!


CO2 Cycle

The CO2 cycle is a natural cycle where carbon is moved around in an ecosystem. Plants take in CO2 through their leaves. This CO2 is used in a process known as photosynthesis, where water, CO2, and sunlight are used to create sugar. This also creates oxygen as a byproduct. Animals like us breathe in the oxygen, then breathe out CO2. The plants use this CO2 again and the cycle repeats.

Plants and animals use these gasses to survive. The process of taking in and letting out gasses like oxygen and CO2 is known as respiration.




Byproduct

A byproduct is a chemical that is left over after some process. This chemical is usually disposed of. For example, when a plant takes in carbon dioxide for photosynthesis, it creates oxygen as a byproduct. This oxygen is released through the plant's leaves.



Global Warming

Global warming is a result of the greenhouse effect. When greenhouse gasses like CO2 and methane are in the atmosphere, they let heat from the sun through, but not back out into space. This is much like a greenhouse, which traps heat inside it. This can heat up the oceans, which causes very drastic weather systems like hurricanes. Just like how the fluid in a thermometer expands when it is heated up, the whole ocean expands when it is heated up. This can cause major floods and trillions of dollars of damage to cities and towns along coastlines.


Greenhouse Effect

The greenhouse effect is a phenomenon where the Earth's atmosphere heats up. This can have very large consuquences. Just like how the fluid in a thermometer expands when it is heated up, when the whole ocean is heated up, even by a tiny amount, it expands, causing massive floods. Because warm water is fuel for hurricanes, hurricanes that feed off of warm ocean water can get really violent.


Greenhouse Gas

A greenhouse gas is any gas that contributes to the greenhouse effect. These gasses heat up the Earth's atmosphere. This is known as global warming. Some examples include methane and carbon dioxide.




Solar Energy

Solar energy is energy that comes from the Sun. Solar cells and solar panels are examples of electronics that generate electricity from light. Plants take in energy from the sun, carbon dioxide, and water to create sugars through a process known as photosynthesis. Solar power is a renewable resource.



Renewable Resource

A renewable resource is a resource that replenishes itself faster than humans can use it. Examples include things like wind energy and solar energy. Things like fossil fuels are not renewable resources. They take millions of years to form. When fossil fuels are burned, ash and carbon dioxide are created as byproducts. They contribute to global warming.



Food Pyramid

A food pyramid is a way to display the flow of energy in an ecosystem. The energy usually starts with the sun. This energy is an abiotic factor. After that, there are usually plants, then some consumer, then some more consumers, etc. It is a pyramid because it represents how there is a lot less energy to go around at the top than there is at the bottom.

Fractions

Fractions in math represent quantities that are rational but aren't a whole number. Fractions like 1/2, 2/3, or even 9127/15769 represent parts of a whole. The denominator is the number of pieces the whole is broken up into. The numerator is the number of pieces total.

4/5 represents 4 pieces out of 5 total pieces.

Egyptians only recognise fractions with a 1 on the top, called aliquot fractions. They also could only repeat each fraction once. So 2/5 was written as 1/5 + 1/6 + 1/30.

The Babylonian system wrote fractions as units and 60ths of a unit, called minute parts. Each minute part is broken into 60 second minute parts. Then third minute parts, then quarter minute parts, etc. This is why an hour is broken into 60 minutes, and minutes into 60 seconds. However, a second is then broken into hundreths of a second instead of sixtieths.

Numerator

The numerator of a fraction is the number on top of a fraction. A fraction multiplied by the denominator of that fraction returns the numerator.



Denominator

The denominator of a fraction is the number on the bottom of a fraction. When the denominator of a fraction is multiplied by the fraction, it returns the numerator of the fraction. When two fractions are multiplied together, the numerator of the resulting fraction is the product of the numerators in both fractions, and the denominator of the resulting fraction is the product of the denominators in both fractions.




The Hairy Ball Theorem

The hairy ball theorem states that on any topologically spherical object, if we were to assign a continuous vector field along the surface of that object, there will always be at least one point where there is a zero vector.

This inplies that say, on the Earth, there is always at least one place where the wind isn't blowing. Where this theorem gets its name is in the case where we have a ball, densely covered in hair. If we try to comb the hair flat, there will always be a tuft.



Topologically Spherical Object

In the field of math called topology, a topologically spherical object is a 3d closed object with zero holes in it.



Continuous Vector Field

In linear algebra, a continuous vector field is a surface where every point is assigned a vector. One example is the direction of wind on the Earth.



Zero Vector

In linear algebra and vector mechanics, a zero vector is a vector that isn't going anywhere. A zero vector is denoted by a bold 0.



Vector

In linear algebra and vector mechanics, a vector is a vertical matrix where each value denotes a set of coordinates that describes where the vector ends up. It is usually represented on a graph as an arrow whose tail is on the origin and tip rests on the coordinates of the vector.

In vector mechanics and physics, a vector can also represent a force.



Origin of a Graph

In most of math, including geometry and algebra, the origin of graph rests on the intersection of two or more axes. It typically represents a zero vector or the coordinates (0,0).

Pure mathematics is a section of mathematics that deals with purely abstract ideas and concepts. However, there is a lot of overlap between pure mathematics and applied mathematics. For example, when Gerolamo Cardano invented complex numbers while trying to find solutions to cubic equations, that same math came in handy when studying things like physics and fluid dynamics.

Very often, a concept in pure mathematics seems so abstract that it shouldn't have very many real applications; however, it often seems that the more abstract the idea, the more pactical uses it has.

Addition

Addition is one of the 4 basic operations of arithmetic; a field of mathematics. The 4 operations are: Addition, Subtraction, Multiplication, and Division. Additionis commonly denoted by the plus symbol (+). Adding two numbers together can be though of breaking each number into that many 1's, then counting the total number of 1's. Saying, "2+3=5" is the same as saying, "2 of something and another 3 of something is five of that something."

A common example in textbooks is using apples as units; "2 apples plus another 2 apples is 4 apples total."

Addidtion can do more than just count groups of items, it is useful with things like complex numbers, variables, and even matrices.

One of the properties of addition is that it is communative. This means that a+b=b+a. Addition is also associative. This means that if we are adding more than one object together, it doesn't matter what order we add in. This even holds true for quaternions, which are an extension to complex numbers. When multiplying two quaternions together, the order acutally matters. But the order still doesn't matter for addition.

Determinant in Mathematics

In linear algebra, A determinant can be computed from the elements of a square matrix. The determinant of a matrix A is denoted by det(A) The determinant can be computed for a matrix using simple formulas. However, there is a geometric explanation for it.

When visualizing a 2x2 matrix on a plane as a transformation, imagine an object on the plane. The determinant of that transformation is the amount that the area of that object gets scaled after the transformation. If a transformation flips the plane, then we represent that as a negative number.

In 3 dimensions, it is almost the same, except we are dealing with volume instead of area.


Square Matrix

In linear algebra, a square matrix is a matrix where the rows and columns of the matrix are the same. Determinants can only be computed for a square matrix.

Platonic Solid

A platonic solid in n dimensions is a shape made of n-1 platonic solids. 3 dimensional platonic solids are commonly used as dice because they have excessive symmetry. Platonic solids have the property where every face is the same, and every angle between faces are the same; so you can't tell one corner from another.

In zero dimensions, there is only one platonic solid; a point.

In one dimension, there is still only one platonic solid; a line segment.

In two dimensions, there are infinitely many platonic solids; starting at a triangle, then a square, then a pentagon, then a hexagon, then a septagon, etc. The limit of this is a circle, which isn't a platonic solid.

In three dimensions, there are precisely 5 platonic solids;

In four dimensions, there are six platonic solids.

In any dimension after 4 dimensions, there is always exactly 3 platonic solids.

Letter Case

Letter case is the distinction of letters that are in uppercase (majuscule) or lowercase (minuscule) in the written form of a language. In languages that use upper and lower cases for letters, every letter has one upper case representation and one lower case representation. Generally, uppercase letters are larger than lowercase. Each form of a letter is pronounced the same and is organised the same when in alphabetical order. For example, the latin letter "a" is pronounced and treated the same regardless of whether it is uppercase (A) or lowercase (a).

The word "case" in "letter case" comes from older times, when letters were small blocks that were arranged, dipped in ink, then pressed onto a paper to create a document. Each "case" of a letter was stored in one of two physical cases; an "upper" case, and a "lower" case.

In mathematics, a capital letter "a" represents a different object than a small letter "a", unless stated otherwise.



Greek Alphabet

The greek alphabet has been used to write the Greek language scince late 9th century or early 8th century BC. The greek alphabet is derived from the earlier Phoenician alphabet. It was the first language with distinct characters for each letter to have only certain letters be vowels, and others to be only consonants. This language is the ancestor of Latin and Cryllic scripts. It originally only had one case for each letter, but developed letter case at the same time as Latin; aquiring upper and lower cases for each letter.
